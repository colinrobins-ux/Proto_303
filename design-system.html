Visual Design System — v1

The look, feel & language of The Ivy

A single reference for the brand across its three rooms — weddings, the concert hall, and retail. Bolder than before, still unmistakably refined.

Built from the Luxury Venue Website Study  ·  May 2026
01 — Foundation

One brand, three rooms

The Ivy is a single identity expressed across three offerings. The logotype, typefaces, and core palette stay constant. What shifts is tone — each room is allowed its own register, the way rooms in one grand house feel different but belong together.

Room I

Weddings & Events

Warm, romantic, light. Cream grounds, ivy green, soft gold. Copy speaks in second person and sells a feeling.

Room II

The Concert Hall

Dark, charged, dramatic. Forest-black grounds, stage-light gold, the claret accent earns its keep here.

Room III

The Shop

Crisp, editorial, tactile. Paper-white grounds, gold detailing, product photography as the hero.

Positioning in one line: “A storied estate where a wedding, a concert, and a beautifully made object all feel like they belong to the same evening.”

02 — Color

The refined palette

The original palette was right in spirit but quiet in voice. The refinement keeps the neutral foundation and the ivy-green anchor, then sharpens the accents: a richer, more luminous gold, a bolder mid-green for interaction, and one disciplined accent of claret for moments that need to raise their voice.

Before — quiet

Muted ivy green, a soft tan "brass," and a sage that competed with the green. Elegant, but it whispered — accents blended into the neutrals instead of leading.

After — bolder, still refined

A deeper, more saturated ivy. A true luminous gilt in place of the tan. A confident verdigris for links and interaction. And claret — held in reserve — for genuine emphasis.

The ratio — how much of each

Hold the discipline: roughly 70% neutral (paper, cream), 20% ivy/forest green, 8% gilt, and 2% claret. Claret is bold precisely because it is rare — if it appears more than once or twice per screen, it stops working.

04 — Typography

Two typefaces, a strict hierarchy

A high-contrast display serif carries the elegance; a clean geometric sans handles everything functional. The pairing is unchanged from the study — it works. Discipline is in the hierarchy, not in adding more fonts.

Display — Cormorant GaramondHeadlines, names, ledes
An estate for
your finest hours

Italic for ledes and pull quotes — warmth without ornament.

Text — JostBody, labels, navigation, UI

— Letter-Spaced Label In Caps —

Jost handles body copy, navigation, buttons, and captions. Set body text at a light weight with generous line height so long-scroll pages stay calm and readable. Labels are set in caps with wide tracking.

The type scale
Display / 62pxHero headline
H2 / 38pxSection heading
H3 / 24pxSubsection heading
Lede / 22px italicAn introductory line
Body / 15pxStandard paragraph copy
Label / 12px capsKicker label
Caption / 12pxImage caption & fine print

Rule of thumb: serif for anything emotional, sans for anything functional. Never set body copy in the serif; never set a hero headline in the sans. A script face may appear once — a couple's name, a date — never as running text.

05 — Layout & spacing

Space is the luxury

Generous margins and few elements per screen are what read as expensive. The grid and spacing scale exist to protect that emptiness.

Spacing scale — an 8px base
8px — tight gaps, inside small components
16px — default gap between related elements
24px — card padding, paragraph spacing
48px — page side margins, gap between blocks
72px — vertical padding between full sections
120px — breathing room around hero & CTA bands
Grid & containers

Content max-width 1080px, centered, with 48px side margins. A 12-column grid underneath; most layouts use 1, 2, or 3 columns. Full-bleed heroes and image bands break the container edge-to-edge. Mobile collapses everything to a single column and drops side margins to 24px.

07 — Imagery

Photography direction

Imagery carries these pages — the study was unambiguous about it. The system below is what the eventual shoot should deliver.

Commission this
  • Real shots of the actual property and real events — never stock
  • One consistent warm grade across every frame; natural, golden-hour light
  • A mix of wide (sells scale), close detail (sells craft), and slow ambient video (sells atmosphere)
  • Tight curation — few, exceptional images over a full archive
  • For the concert hall: low-light, stage-glow, the room at night
Avoid this
  • Stock photography of anonymous venues — it reads as budget instantly
  • Mixed grades — phone snaps beside professional shots
  • Dumping every photo into one undifferentiated grid
  • Heavy filters, cool blue casts, or trend-driven color treatments
  • Text baked into images — keep type live and accessible
08 — Voice

How The Ivy speaks

Addressed to “you.” Confident, unhurried, never pushy. Sells the feeling first and proves it with one specific, named detail. The copy in the wireframes follows these rules.

Use — earned, when true

Vocabulary that still carries weight when backed by a real detail.

storiedthe grounds a celebrationan affair inquireartistry intimateyour story

Retire — or earn hard

Now table-stakes across every competitor — nearly invisible.

elevatedcurated timelessbespoke effortlessexperiential unforgettableluxury (stated)

The test: if a sentence would work word-for-word on a competitor's site, it isn't saying anything yet. Replace the adjective with a fact — a year, a name, a number, a tree.

09 — In short

Hold the line on these

Do
  • Let one idea own each screen — emptiness reads as confidence
  • Lead with a full-bleed hero: one image, one line, one invitation
  • Keep claret rare — that is the entire source of its power
  • Pair serif for emotion, sans for function — no third typeface
  • Write to “you,” and back every claim with a specific detail
Don't
  • Crowd a screen with competing calls to action
  • Let gilt and claret appear at equal weight — gilt leads
  • Use “Book Now” — the inquiry is the first act of service
  • Reach for buzzwords in place of substance and proof
  • Introduce pure white, cool greys, or off-system color

Luxury online is restraint, real beauty, and a confident, quiet voice.
